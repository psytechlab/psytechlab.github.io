
Under this banner, February passed. We are developing two models: the first model should identify various signals and circumstances that increase the possibility of suicide, while the second model identifies restraining factors. And with the second model, everything was very bad: it gave an f1_macro of 55, when we had declared a minimum of 70.

We expected that the anti-suicidal model would be worse than the pre-suicidal model (that's what we call the two sisters). When we labeled the data within the team, colleagues noted that anti-suicidal labeling was more difficult, despite having 4 times fewer classes than pre-suicidal. But to such an extent - this was overkill. Looking at how the data was labeled, at the feedback that labelers gave us, at the fact that we ourselves sometimes cannot decide where a text should be classified, we decided to rebuild the classes and completely re-label the anti-suicidal dataset.

We rebuilt the classes according to this scheme:
1. Sample several dozen examples from each class.
2. Two task "stakeholders" label the main message of the text (aka what the author wanted to say).
3. Labeled messages are reduced to closed lists.
4. Lists from different "stakeholders" are combined.
5. Individual messages are grouped into new classes.

What's nice about this scheme is that besides the classes themselves, we automatically get clear class features in the form of a list of messages, as well as examples that fully cover these features.

As a result of this work, we got a class for expressing love. This includes texts where people write about sympathy, admiration, or, actually, love for other people and animals. This obvious class wasn't in the original description. We automatically assigned it to the "presence of positive social connections" class. Here we smoothly transition to another problem of the old version: time description. Should the text "I had a boyfriend whom I loved to distraction" belong to a class whose name starts with the word "presence"? Labelers answered this question differently. Now everything about love - whether in the past or present - goes into the love class, while the social connections class emphasizes the word "positive" also regardless of time. The time problem existed in other classes too, which we also managed to solve.

Since we had spent the main labeling budget, we could no longer afford to label with triple overlap. To be sure that quality remains at the same level, we check each labeler every few hundred examples and immediately give feedback. This, of course, adds operational work for us, but we immediately get a verification set. The process is still ongoing, but we've already tried training a model on what we have now. Cutting off very small classes, we got f1_macro quality of 0.71 on 30 percent of what was labeled. This is a victory. We need to at least maintain quality at the same level.

Pre-suicidal data can't be re-labeled so easily - there are 40 thousand examples. If you look at the confusion matrix, you can see that there are good classes and bad ones. The left stripe, which shouldn't be there, catches the eye. This is our irrelevant class somehow getting confused with all the others.

After analyzing incorrectly predicted examples, we noted the following problems:
* The third-person rule is violated - if something bad is said in the text not about the author, then we record such texts as irrelevant, for example "he told me that he dreams of quick death".
* There are complex examples that require "taking a logical step" to classify them into the appropriate class, but BERTs can't do that. Example: "the girl found me lying in the bathtub with a knife in my hands".
* Texts falling under multiple classes - initially we collapsed multiple classes into one by importance priority. We didn't expect it to work well, and that's how it turned out.
* Some examples in the test are lexically simply not covered by the training data, so the model doesn't even know that such things are possible.
* Labeling errors, of course.

The main question: how do we select the problematic data? Experience suggests one brilliant method - dataset cartography. The methodology allows you to distribute examples into those that are well learned by your specific model, and those that are not so well learned. Often, examples from the second group are just labeled crookedly. You want to ask, what do maps have to do with it? Because the result is an addictive graph, like the one below. More details about the method and interpretation can be found in this [Habr post](https://habr.com/ru/companies/mts_ai/articles/825090/). And we're interested in examples that lie in the "hard-to-learn" area.

![cartography](/assets/images/data_cartography_presui.png)

In total, we managed to select about 10 thousand examples. And here are the top-5 classes that lie in that area:
```
Irrelevant                                                                                      1726
Anti-suicidal signal                                                                           992
Feelings/spiritual emptiness, depression, melancholy, sadness                                  860
Feelings/negative self-perception, guilt, shame, worthlessness, self-flagellation             763
Feelings/helplessness, hopelessness, despair                                                  710
```
We caught the bull by the horns. With the anti-suicidal class, everything is already clear, but the batch of irrelevant class - a class that really spreads strongly across the others - indeed contains either incorrect labeling or violation of the third-person rule. By the way, half of all selected data are originally multi-intents. It turns out we found what we were looking for.

Now we're working on how we can further enrich this sample to fit into our emaciated budget, plus we're still analyzing multi-intent examples. We noticed that there are recurring "scenarios" that can quite well be separated into a separate class. For example, in anti-suicidal texts you can very often find texts like "I want to die, but I feel sorry for my mom". This text contains two classes: thoughts about death and positive social interconnections (it's positive because the author worries about another person's feelings, which means this person is not indifferent to them).
